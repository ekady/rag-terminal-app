THE MISFORTUNES OF MISS ELEANOR VANE
A Novel in Three Volumes

By the Author of Several Unfinished Attempts at Propriety

~ ~ ~

VOLUME THE FIRST

CHAPTER I

It is a truth, if not universally acknowledged, then at least generally suspected among the residents of Ashford-upon-Wye, that a young woman of moderate fortune and excellent sense is in no immediate want of a husband — though this opinion was held by none so firmly, and defended with such cheerful obstinacy, as by Miss Eleanor Vane herself. Her neighbours, naturally, thought otherwise.

The village of Ashford-upon-Wye sat comfortably in the vale of a gentle Herefordshire valley, surrounded by hedgerows of considerable antiquity and the sort of rolling green hills that painters are always attempting and never quite capturing. The houses were old enough to be interesting and new enough to be habitable, which is as fine a combination as one may hope for in English architecture. At the centre of the village stood the church of Saint Agatha, its modest spire pointing heavenward with the confidence of an institution that has never seriously doubted its own importance.

The Vane family occupied Whitmore Cottage — a name that was, strictly speaking, an act of architectural optimism, for the building contained eleven rooms, a walled kitchen garden, two staircases (one principal, one used chiefly by the cook, who had strong opinions about the propriety of sharing stairs with the family she served), and a library that had been accumulating books since the reign of George II without any corresponding effort at organisation. It was the sort of library that revealed, upon close examination, a great deal about the reading habits, enthusiasms, and abandoned projects of several generations of Vanes. There were books on botany opened to the same page for forty years, novels whose endings had never been reached, and at least one volume of sermons that had never been opened at all, having been purchased for the sake of appearances during a period of particular social anxiety in 1773.

Miss Eleanor Vane was the eldest of three daughters. She was eight-and-twenty, which her mother considered an age of moderate alarm, and which Eleanor herself considered an age of considerable freedom. She had dark eyes that missed very little, a quick mind that sometimes said things before consulting her manners, and a gift for observation that she had learned, over many years, to deploy with some discretion. She was not handsome in the conventional sense that would have satisfied a portrait painter, but she was interesting, which is a quality that lasts considerably longer and causes rather more trouble.

Her sister Margaret, who was five-and-twenty, was widely considered the beauty of the family, a distinction she wore with the careless grace of someone who has never been required to develop a personality to compensate for its absence. She had golden hair, blue eyes, and a laugh that could be heard from the rectory garden on still evenings. She was kind, somewhat impractical, and possessed of a romantic disposition that led her to read entirely too many novels and draw entirely too many conclusions from them.

The youngest sister, Cecily, was nineteen, and at that particular stage of girlhood when the world seems simultaneously too small and too large, and everything that happens is either desperately boring or overwhelmingly significant, with very little in between. She was clever in the intermittent, unpredictable way that parents find both encouraging and exhausting, and she had recently announced an intention to learn Greek, which no one in the family had taken seriously except Cecily herself and the local curate, Mr. Prentiss, who had offered his assistance and was subsequently unsure whether he had done something praiseworthy or merely inconvenient.

Mrs. Vane, the mother of this trio, was a woman whose nerves had been much commented upon for so many years that they had become almost a member of the household in their own right. She spoke of them as one might speak of a demanding relative — with a mixture of affection, resignation, and mild resentment. She had been a handsome woman in her youth and was a fretful one in her middle age, occupied chiefly with the business of securing her daughters' futures and regretting her inability to accomplish this with greater speed. She was not a foolish woman, though she sometimes permitted urgency to overwhelm judgement, and she loved her daughters with a completeness that occasionally made her impossible.

Mr. Vane, who had inherited a modest but sufficient income from a prudent grandfather, spent the better part of each day in his library, emerging at meals to offer observations of mild irony about the world beyond his windows, and retreating again with the contentment of a man who has arranged his life exactly as he wishes it. He was fond of Eleanor with a particular warmth, recognising in her something of his own constitution — the preference for truth over comfort, the appreciation of the ridiculous, the underlying seriousness that conceals itself behind an agreeable manner.

It was into this domestic arrangement, comfortable and familiar as a well-worn chair, that the news arrived one bright morning in October that Hatherswick Park had at last been let.

CHAPTER II

Hatherswick Park was the great house of the neighbourhood — great in the architectural sense, occupying the high ground above the valley with the assurance of a building that has never been required to apologise for its size. It had stood empty for three years following the death of old Sir Edmund Carew, whose estate had passed to a nephew in Derbyshire who found Herefordshire inconveniently far from everything he considered important. The nephew, after some deliberation, had decided to let the property rather than sell it, and the neighbourhood had been in a state of suspended anticipation ever since, wondering who would come to fill its many rooms and whether they would prove an improvement on their predecessors.

The news was brought to Whitmore Cottage by Mrs. Fortescue, who lived half a mile down the lane and who had an unparalleled gift for arriving with information at the precise moment when it would cause the greatest sensation. She appeared at ten o'clock in the morning, slightly out of breath from the walk and the effort of containing her intelligence, and was shown into the parlour where Mrs. Vane and Eleanor were engaged in the domestic rituals of the morning — the former engaged in correspondence, the latter in reading a volume that she set aside with the unhurried manner of someone who knows she will return to it.

"My dear Mrs. Vane," said Mrs. Fortescue, settling herself into the best chair with the ease of long familiarity, "I have the most extraordinary intelligence. Hatherswick Park is let at last."

Mrs. Vane set down her pen immediately. "Is it indeed? And to whom?"

"A Mr. Ashby. From London, I understand, though originally from Shropshire. A single gentleman of very good fortune — thirty thousand a year at the very least, though Colonel Fortescue suspects it may be rather more. He is to take possession at the end of the month."

"Single!" said Mrs. Vane, with a warmth that caused Eleanor to look up from the middle distance where she had been contemplating the wallpaper.

"Entirely single," confirmed Mrs. Fortescue. "And I am told — though I cannot vouch for this with absolute certainty — that he is not yet five-and-thirty, and quite agreeable in his person."

"Eleanor," said Mrs. Vane, turning to her eldest daughter with the expression of a general who has just received news of reinforcements, "do you hear this?"

"I hear it perfectly, Mama," said Eleanor. "A gentleman has taken a house in the neighbourhood. I trust the house will suit him."

"The house will suit him! I wonder at you, Eleanor. You are eight-and-twenty."

"I am well aware of my own age."

"Then you will understand," said Mrs. Vane, with the patience of a woman who has had this conversation many times, "that this is a matter of some consequence."

"I understand that a Mr. Ashby has taken Hatherswick Park," said Eleanor. "Whether this is a matter of consequence to our family must depend on many things as yet unknown. His character, for instance. His understanding. Whether he is the sort of man whose company one might actually enjoy across the span of a lifetime, or merely across an evening."

Mrs. Fortescue looked mildly alarmed, as she always did when Eleanor said sensible things too clearly.

"You are very particular," said Mrs. Vane.

"I hope to be," said Eleanor pleasantly. "It seems the minimum requirement."

CHAPTER III

Mr. Ashby arrived at Hatherswick Park on the twenty-eighth of October, on a morning of pale sunshine and considerable local interest. Eleanor, walking with Cecily along the lane that skirted the park wall, observed the carriage at a distance and thought very little of it beyond noting that it was well-appointed and drawn by four horses, which suggested either prosperity or a fondness for inconvenient speed.

"That will be Mr. Ashby," said Cecily, stopping to observe.

"Very probably," said Eleanor, walking on.

"You are determined not to be curious about him."

"I am determined to be curious about things in proportion to their importance," said Eleanor. "At present, Mr. Ashby is a stranger whose principal distinction is that he has taken a large house near our own. This does not, in my view, constitute grounds for the sort of excitement currently prevalent in the neighbourhood."

"Margaret is tremendously excited."

"Margaret is tremendously excited by most things. It is one of her great charms."

Cecily was quiet for a moment, matching her step to her sister's. "Do you think he will prove interesting?"

"I have no idea. He may prove quite dull. He may prove quite the reverse. The difficulty with people is that there is no way of knowing until you have actually spoken to them, and sometimes not even then."

"That is rather a discouraging view."

"I think it is rather a hopeful one," said Eleanor. "It means every new person is an open question."

The first meeting with Mr. Ashby was accomplished at the Mercer assembly, held in the town of Ludbury five miles distant, where the neighbourhood gathered periodically to dance, observe one another, and draw conclusions that kept them in conversation for weeks afterward. Eleanor attended with her mother and sisters, wearing a white muslin gown with a green ribbon that she had chosen herself on the grounds that it was becoming without being showy, which was precisely the balance she preferred.

Mr. Ashby arrived with a friend — a Mr. Dalton, who was announced as his particular companion and who was immediately assessed by the assembled company as a secondary consideration, however pleasant he might prove individually. The primary object of interest was Mr. Ashby himself, and Eleanor, who had been determined to observe him with the disinterested attention of a naturalist, found herself obliged to admit that he was more engaging than she had expected.

He was tall, and carried himself without the self-consciousness that great height sometimes produces in men who have spent their lives being measured against doorframes. He had a direct way of looking at people that Eleanor initially mistrusted — direct gazes are sometimes a performance rather than a natural condition — but which, as the evening progressed, seemed to be genuine. He spoke with intelligence, laughed without loudness, and — this Eleanor noted with particular attention — listened with the same care he brought to speaking, which is a quality rarer than it ought to be.

He asked Eleanor to dance during the second set, having been introduced by the master of ceremonies with the smooth efficiency that such gentlemen bring to their professional duties.

"You are the eldest Miss Vane," he said, as they took their places. It was not a question.

"I am," said Eleanor. "And you are the tenant of Hatherswick Park, about whom the neighbourhood has been speculating for three weeks."

Something shifted in his expression — not displeasure, but amusement. "I am. I hope the speculation has not been too unkind."

"On the contrary. You have been assigned every virtue. I would caution you against attempting to live up to all of them."

"That is frank advice."

"It is practical advice. Expectations formed in the absence of information are almost always disappointed, since they bear very little relationship to reality. It is better to allow people to discover what you actually are."

Mr. Ashby considered this as they moved through the figures of the dance. "And what are you discovering?"

"That you dance well," said Eleanor, "and that you are not afraid of an honest remark. Both are more than I had strictly counted on."

He smiled at that — a genuine smile, not a social one — and Eleanor noted that this, too, was a point in his favour.

CHAPTER IV

The weeks that followed the Mercer assembly were busy ones in the social life of Ashford-upon-Wye. Mr. Ashby and Mr. Dalton called at Whitmore Cottage within three days, which was generally agreed to be appropriately prompt without being desperately eager. They were received in the drawing room by Mrs. Vane, who had been preparing for this moment with considerable internal ceremony, and by all three sisters, who had been told to be at home in the afternoon with as little ceremony as Mrs. Vane could manage.

The call lasted nearly an hour and a half, which exceeded the usual duration by a full sixty minutes and was considered a significant indicator by everyone in the room except Eleanor, who considered it merely a pleasant conversation, and Mr. Dalton, who had fallen into a discussion of botany with Cecily and had quite lost track of the time.

Mr. Dalton was a pleasant surprise. He was three-and-thirty, had a comfortable estate in Shropshire, and possessed the sort of good humour that is not contingent on circumstances — an even-tempered cheerfulness that Eleanor found genuinely restful to be near. He had been a friend of Mr. Ashby's since their schooldays and spoke of him with the unguarded affection that is only possible between people who have known each other across many years and several varieties of difficulty.

"You have been friends a long time," Eleanor observed to Mr. Dalton, during a moment when Mr. Ashby had been drawn into conversation with her mother.

"Since Eton," said Mr. Dalton. "Ashby was two years above me, but we were in the same house and found ourselves in agreement on most of the things that mattered. Books, principally. And the general absurdity of many of the rules."

"He strikes me as a serious man."

"He is," said Mr. Dalton, "in the best sense of that word — he takes things seriously that deserve to be taken seriously, and does not burden himself with the others. He can be very amusing when the occasion suits."

"And when the occasion does not suit?"

"Then he is quiet, and people sometimes mistake quiet for coldness. It is not coldness. It is simply the economy of a man who does not see the point of speaking unless he has something worth saying."

Eleanor glanced across the room to where Mr. Ashby was listening to her mother's account of some neighbourhood matter — listening, she noted, with genuine attention, not the performed attention of a man enduring a social obligation.

"You are recommending him to me," she said.

Mr. Dalton looked momentarily caught. "I am giving you an honest account of him," he said. "What you do with it is entirely your own affair."

"A diplomatic reply," said Eleanor. "Though in this case I suspect you mean exactly what you say."

"With Ashby," said Mr. Dalton, "I generally mean exactly what I say. He has had that effect on me for twenty years."

CHAPTER V

There are, in every neighbourhood, certain mechanisms by which the social life perpetuates itself — dinners, assemblies, morning calls, and the occasional private concert at which someone's daughter performs adequately and is applauded with generous inaccuracy. Ashford-upon-Wye had all of these, and the arrival of two new gentlemen at Hatherswick Park added a pleasant freshness to these familiar forms.

Eleanor found herself in company with Mr. Ashby with a frequency that she attributed to the smallness of the neighbourhood and that her mother attributed to Providence. They met at the Fortescues' dinner, where they were seated near enough to speak and found themselves engaged in a discussion of whether the countryside or the city better suited sustained thought — Eleanor defending the country on the grounds of quiet, Ashby defending the city on the grounds of variety, both arguing, Eleanor suspected, positions they did not entirely hold, simply because the exercise was interesting.

"You do not actually prefer the city," she said, toward the end of the dinner.

He looked at her with something between surprise and appreciation. "What makes you say so?"

"The way you described the walk from the park to the village this morning when you met my father. You spoke of it in the way people speak of things they genuinely see, not things they observe out of duty."

"You were not present at that conversation."

"My father mentioned it at luncheon."

A pause. "You listen to what people say about the things they enjoy."

"It is generally more informative than what they say about themselves directly."

He was quiet for a moment. "Then I will tell you that you are correct. I find the country more congenial than London, though London has things I value. The quiet here is different — it is not merely the absence of noise but the presence of something else. I have not yet found the word for it."

"Accumulation," said Eleanor. "Things have had time to accumulate. History, growth, the layering of one season over another. Cities have history too, but it is mostly buried. Out here it is still on the surface."

He looked at her with that directness she had noticed at the assembly. "Yes," he said simply. "That is exactly it."

It was a small exchange. Eleanor catalogued it with the precision she brought to most things and carried it home in the carriage with her mother's voice forming a pleasant background that required no particular attention.

VOLUME THE SECOND

CHAPTER VI

November brought frost to the valley and a development that no one in the neighbourhood had foreseen: the arrival of Miss Constance Harley.

Miss Harley came from London, which in the minds of many local residents was recommendation enough — London people being assumed to have access to superior fashion and more interesting conversation, though these assumptions were not always borne out. She was the niece of Mrs. Fortescue's great friend Mrs. Hadlow, and she arrived for a visit of indefinite duration, bringing with her three trunks, an abigail of unusual competence, and the sort of self-possession that a very pretty woman sometimes acquires from a long familiarity with being found attractive.

She was, objectively, beautiful. Eleanor observed this without particular emotion, the way one observes a very fine painting — acknowledging its quality without feeling personally implicated. Miss Harley had dark hair, pale skin, a graceful figure, and an ease of manner that was either natural or very well-practised, and which produced much the same effect either way.

She was introduced to the neighbourhood at Mrs. Hadlow's dinner, and she was seated next to Mr. Ashby, which happened to be because of the arrangement of the table rather than any particular intention on Mrs. Hadlow's part — though everyone present formed their own conclusions.

Eleanor, seated further down the table beside Mr. Dalton, observed without appearing to observe. This was a skill she had developed over many years and had mixed feelings about, since it was undeniably useful and also, if she examined it too carefully, a little solitary.

"Miss Harley is very beautiful," said Mr. Dalton beside her, quietly enough to be a private observation.

"She is," agreed Eleanor.

"Ashby will find her very easy to talk to," he added, in a tone that might have been neutral. "She has a talent for putting people at ease."

Eleanor turned her attention fully to Mr. Dalton and found in his expression something that was either concern or curiosity — she could not quite determine which. "You are telling me something," she said.

"I am making conversation," he said, equably.

"You are making the sort of conversation that contains more information than is strictly necessary for sociability," said Eleanor. "But I shall not press you."

She did not press him, and the dinner continued, and Miss Harley's laugh rose above the general conversation at intervals, and Mr. Ashby was seen to be entirely engaged in the discourse beside him. Eleanor ate her pheasant and spoke to the rector on her left about the new system of crop rotation being attempted by one of the local farmers, and found the conversation genuinely interesting, and told herself that this was entirely satisfactory.

CHAPTER VII

The winter was mild for Herefordshire, which meant that outdoor occupations remained possible past the usual point of seasonal surrender, and walking parties of various compositions became a regular feature of the neighbourhood's sociability. Eleanor walked most mornings along the lane toward the common, sometimes with Cecily — who had commenced her Greek lessons with Mr. Prentiss and had progressed to the alphabet with a determination that was beginning to alarm him — and sometimes alone, which she preferred, though she would not have said so to her mother.

On a morning in late November, she encountered Mr. Ashby at the stile that marked the boundary between the Whitmore meadow and the common ground. He was coming from the direction of the park, and had about him the appearance of a man who has been walking for some time with no particular destination — a content, purposeful aimlessness that Eleanor recognised in herself.

"Miss Vane," he said, not appearing surprised, though she could not determine whether this was because he had seen her coming or because he was the sort of man who did not perform surprise.

"Mr. Ashby. You are out early."

"I am always out early. The house has many rooms and most of them I have not yet determined a use for. Outdoors, the problem does not arise."

She smiled at that. "How do you find Hatherswick? Now that you have had some weeks to settle."

He considered. He had a habit of considering questions before answering them that Eleanor found entirely admirable, living as she did among people who tended to speak first and think afterward. "I find it very fine," he said at last. "And very large, in the way that emphasises rather than alleviates solitude."

"You are used to smaller quarters?"

"I have lived in London these past several years, in rooms that were sufficient for a single person and entirely unsuited to any other arrangement. Hatherswick was my grandfather's house — I spent summers here as a boy and loved it without fully understanding it. I thought perhaps, now that I am older and have a better understanding of what I want from a life, I might understand it better."

"And do you?"

He looked across the common, where the morning mist had not yet cleared from the hollows. "I understand it differently. Whether better is yet to be determined."

They walked together along the edge of the common, not by explicit agreement but by the natural logic of two people whose direction happened to coincide. The conversation moved without effort through several territories — the management of the estate, which he was approaching with a conscientious attention to the people dependent on it; the village, which he was coming to know; the neighbourhood, which he found more interesting than he had expected.

"You speak of the neighbourhood as though you had expected to find it dull," said Eleanor.

"I expected to find it restful," he said. "Which is different. Dull is the absence of interest. Restful is the presence of a different kind of interest — slower, more considered. I had spent several years in a world that moved very quickly and rewarded very loud assertions. I came here partly to hear something quieter."

"And do you hear it?"

He glanced at her sidelong — a glance that was not quite a smile but had something of the same quality. "I begin to," he said.

They reached the point at which the path divided, and parted with the ease of people whose conversation has ended naturally rather than merely stopped, and Eleanor walked home through the pale winter light and thought about the difference between restful and dull, and found she was smiling without quite knowing when she had begun.

CHAPTER VIII

"Eleanor," said Margaret, appearing at the door of the library one afternoon, "you are reading."

"Observant," said Eleanor, without looking up.

"You are always reading. Can you not come and walk with me? I have something to tell you and I cannot tell it properly to someone who is reading."

Eleanor set down her book. Margaret's face had the particular illuminated quality that it acquired during periods of significant emotional engagement, and Eleanor, who knew her sister well, understood at once that the something to be told was not a small thing.

"Sit down," she said. "And tell me."

Margaret sat. She arranged herself on the window seat with the unconscious grace that was simply her way of being, and looked at her hands, and then at Eleanor, and then at her hands again. "Mr. Dalton has spoken to me," she said.

Eleanor waited.

"He has — he has spoken to me of his feelings. Of his — Eleanor, he has asked me." She looked up with eyes that were very bright. "He has asked Papa, and now he has asked me, and I — I said yes, and it is quite the most astonishing thing that has ever happened."

Eleanor crossed the room and took her sister's hands. "And do you love him?" she asked, with the directness that was always her way, modulated now by the particular gentleness she kept for Margaret.

"I think I do," said Margaret. "I know I am not always reliable in these matters — I have thought I was in love before and been entirely wrong — but this feels different. It feels — solid. Like something that will hold." She looked at Eleanor with an expression that was half joy and half uncertainty. "Do you think it will hold?"

"I think," said Eleanor carefully, "that Mr. Dalton is a good man, which is a better foundation than most people begin with. I think he is steady and kind and genuinely values what you are, not simply what you look like. Whether it will hold depends on both of you, over time. But the materials seem sound."

Margaret laughed — the clear, carrying laugh that reached the rectory garden — and embraced her sister with a warmth that left Eleanor slightly breathless. "You always say the most practical things at the most important moments," she said. "It is both reassuring and faintly maddening."

"One does one's best," said Eleanor.

She was genuinely happy for her sister — this required no performance. Mr. Dalton was everything she had said: steady, kind, possessed of a good mind and a good heart and the particular virtue of making the people around him feel at ease. Margaret would be happy. Eleanor believed this with the conviction she reserved for things she had thought through carefully.

And yet, driving home from the Fortescues' that evening, with the news of the engagement settled in the neighbourhood like a stone in water, sending out its small rings of congratulation and comment, Eleanor sat by the dark carriage window and felt something she declined to name precisely — something to do with the word solid, and the phrase something that will hold, and the question of whether she had been right, all these years, to hold herself to such a high standard of certainty before allowing herself to feel anything at all.

CHAPTER IX

The engagement of Miss Margaret Vane to Mr. Dalton was welcomed by the neighbourhood with the warmth reserved for events that confirm everyone's best instincts. Mrs. Vane was delighted in the particular way of mothers who have achieved one objective and can now focus their considerable energies on the next. Mr. Vane expressed his satisfaction in a brief speech over dinner and then returned to his library, which was his way of saying that he was very pleased indeed. Cecily announced that she had always thought Mr. Dalton admirable and that she hoped he did not expect her to abandon her Greek lessons, which no one had suggested, but which she apparently felt should be made clear.

Mr. Ashby called to offer his congratulations with a warmth that spoke well of his friendship with Dalton and his growing attachment to the neighbourhood. He stayed for tea, and in the course of a conversation that wandered pleasantly through several subjects, he mentioned that he was expecting some friends from London the following week.

"Old acquaintances," he said, in a tone that was careful enough to cause Eleanor to notice that it was careful. "A Mr. and Mrs. Wentworth, and Mrs. Wentworth's sister, Miss Hargreave."

"We shall look forward to meeting them," said Mrs. Vane.

"They are very agreeable people," said Mr. Ashby, with the slight emphasis of a man who is saying something accurate while conscious that it does not quite convey everything relevant.

It was Eleanor's experience that when people described others as very agreeable, they were sometimes describing genuine agreeableness, and sometimes describing the quality of being the sort of person one can spend time with without incident, which is a different thing entirely. She filed this observation without acting on it and went on listening.

The Wentworths arrived on a Wednesday. They were a handsome couple in their middle thirties, clearly at ease in the world and with each other, and they moved through the neighbourhood's social life with the fluid confidence of people accustomed to going everywhere and being well received. Mrs. Wentworth was clever and observant; Mr. Wentworth was amiable and said intelligent things occasionally in the manner of someone who has learned to be strategically brief.

Miss Hargreave was twenty-two, and posed an immediate problem to Eleanor's careful system of charitable assumptions. She was not disagreeable. She was, if anything, extremely pleasant — quick, lively, well-read, with a frankness of manner that Eleanor would ordinarily have appreciated. The problem was that she arrived with a history — a history that Mr. Dalton, with rather more forthright friendship than strict policy might recommend, confided to Eleanor during a walk one morning.

"Ashby and Miss Hargreave were nearly engaged," he said. "Two years ago. It was — there were difficulties. Differences of view about what they each wanted from a life. The attachment was real, I think, on both sides. But it came apart."

Eleanor absorbed this with the outward composure she was very good at maintaining. "And now she is here," she said.

"Now she is here," agreed Mr. Dalton. "I do not know what it means. I am not certain Ashby knows what it means."

"It may mean nothing," said Eleanor. "People have histories. Those histories do not necessarily reassert themselves."

"No," said Mr. Dalton. "They do not necessarily."

The qualification hung in the winter air between them like breath made visible, and they walked on in a silence that was companionable and yet somehow charged, in the way that silences are when two people are both thinking hard about the same thing without saying so.

VOLUME THE THIRD

CHAPTER X

Christmas at Ashford-upon-Wye was a thing of considerable domestic ceremony. The church was decorated, the neighbourhood called upon one another with gifts of preserves and fine words, and Whitmore Cottage was filled for three days with the concentrated warmth of a family at ease with itself. Even the nerves of Mrs. Vane took a holiday, subdued by the pleasant reality of Margaret's happiness and the general goodwill of the season.

Eleanor spent the holiday in the state of productive occupation that was her best defence against moods she considered irrational — she read, she managed household matters that her mother left in her hands, she played at cards with her father on the evenings when the house was quiet, and she thought, with the careful precision she brought to everything, about the question of her own heart.

This was not a subject she had entirely avoided before, but she had approached it in the abstract, as a philosophical matter — the question of what she would want, if she wanted something, which she had always been uncertain about. Now she approached it more concretely, which was more uncomfortable.

She knew what she felt when she spoke to Mr. Ashby. She was honest enough with herself to know this, and had been honest enough to name it, at least internally, as something more than the appreciation of an intelligent person for another intelligent person. It was engagement of a particular kind — the sense of being fully present, of finding the world more interesting in his company than out of it, of feeling that her best self was the one that emerged when there was someone capable of meeting it.

She also knew what she did not know. She did not know what he felt. She had learned, over eight-and-twenty years, to be very cautious about inferring feeling from conduct, having observed, in the neighbourhood and in novels and in the general record of human behaviour, how easily one could mistake interest for attachment, and attention for intention. Mr. Ashby was interested in her — this she was nearly certain of. Whether this interest was the kind that settles into something permanent, or the kind that passes with novelty, she could not determine.

And there was Miss Hargreave, who remained at Hatherswick until after Christmas, and who moved through the same social spaces as Eleanor with a pleasant directness that made disliking her entirely impossible and found Eleanor, most mornings, having to exert a small but definite effort of will to be as fair-minded as she preferred to be.

On the day after Christmas — a day of iron skies and frozen puddles — Eleanor walked out early, alone, in the direction of the common. She had been walking for perhaps twenty minutes when she heard footsteps behind her and turned to find Mr. Ashby, in a greatcoat the colour of a winter sky, making his way along the path at a pace that suggested he had been walking quickly.

"Miss Vane," he said, when he had drawn level. He was slightly out of breath, which, for a man of his constitution, suggested he had been walking with some urgency.

"Mr. Ashby. You are abroad early."

"I walked to the common and saw you some way ahead of me," he said. "I increased my pace."

This was direct enough that Eleanor was quiet for a moment, considering it. "Shall we walk?" she said.

They walked. The morning was very still, the frost on the grass catching what pale light there was and holding it. They spoke for a little while about nothing in particular — the cold, the plans for the New Year, the progress of the estate — and then Mr. Ashby stopped, in the way of a man who has been working up to something and has decided that the time for working up to it has passed.

"Miss Vane," he said. "I want to speak to you plainly, if I may."

"You generally do," said Eleanor. "It is one of the things I value in you."

"I know that my position is — there are aspects of it that may give you reason for hesitation. Miss Hargreave is here, and I am aware that her presence raises questions about my own history and my own character that I cannot expect you to simply dismiss." He paused. "I want to tell you, plainly, that what I felt two years ago was real but insufficient — insufficient to the demands of the life I wanted to build, and insufficient to the demands she had for her own life. We were not wrong to part. I have not spent the time since then in grief, but in the gradual and somewhat belated process of understanding what I actually want."

Eleanor held herself very still. The frost, the pale light, the silence of the winter morning.

"And what is it," she said carefully, "that you have determined you want?"

He looked at her with the directness that had been, from the beginning, both the thing she most valued and the thing she found most difficult — because with such a gaze, there is nowhere to hide, and one's own expression does corresponding service.

"A companion," he said. "In the fullest sense of that word. Someone whose mind I cannot predict and whose company I cannot exhaust. Someone who will tell me the truth when it is inconvenient, who has a better opinion of people than circumstances always warrant, and who — " He stopped. "I am speaking very generally."

"You are," said Eleanor. "I notice you have not said anything that is not equally applicable to a great number of people."

"I am trying to be careful," he said. "I do not wish to be presumptuous."

"Being careful to the point of obscurity is its own kind of presumption," said Eleanor. "It assumes that the person you are speaking to cannot bear plainness."

A silence. The frost. The pale light.

"I am in love with you," said Mr. Ashby. "I have been in love with you since the assembly at Mercer, when you told me that expectations formed in the absence of information were almost always disappointed, and I thought: here is someone who is entirely honest, and the world would be considerably better if there were more of it."

Eleanor looked at him for a long moment — at the directness of him, the steadiness, the slight tension in his jaw that told her that this plainness had cost him something, which made it the more valuable.

"I should tell you," she said, "that I have been engaged, for some time, in what I considered a dispassionate analysis of my own feelings, with the intention of arriving at a reliable conclusion before taking any action."

"And have you arrived at one?"

"I find," said Eleanor, "that analysis, however thorough, eventually reaches a point beyond which only experience can take you. And that I am — that I have been — I am in love with you too. I am saying this with less elegance than the feeling deserves, but I believe it to be true."

He reached for her hand, and she gave it, and they stood for a moment in the frost and the pale light of the winter morning with the common stretching out around them, and the village beyond, and the accumulated weight of everything that had come before this point.

"I should speak to your father," he said.

"He is in the library," said Eleanor. "He almost always is. He will be pleased, I think. He considers you a serious man in the best sense of the word."

"Your sister said something similar, earlier in the season."

"We have been discussing you," said Eleanor. "Did you think we hadn't?"

He laughed — the genuine laugh she had heard rarely enough that it still surprised her with its warmth. "I had hoped you were," he said.

CHAPTER XI

The news of Miss Eleanor Vane's engagement to Mr. Ashby of Hatherswick Park was received by the neighbourhood with the satisfaction accorded to outcomes that everyone had privately expected and publicly doubted. Mrs. Vane was so thoroughly delighted that her nerves forgot themselves entirely for a full week. Mr. Vane emerged from the library on the day of the announcement, shook Mr. Ashby's hand with an attention that was a pronouncement in itself, and said only that he was very glad, which was everything.

Cecily declared it perfectly rational and then burst into tears, which Eleanor found both endearing and slightly mysterious. Margaret, whose own happiness was already considerable, found room for more and embraced her sister with a completeness of feeling that required no words.

Miss Hargreave, who had left Hatherswick the week before, sent a note of congratulation that was gracious and contained no undercurrents, which Eleanor thought well of and suspected had cost something.

Mrs. Fortescue, who had brought the original intelligence of Mr. Ashby's arrival and felt a proprietary satisfaction in the conclusion it had reached, told everyone in the neighbourhood that she had always thought how it would be.

And in the library of Whitmore Cottage, on a wet afternoon in January, Eleanor sat with her father over a game of chess and spoke very little, finding that the particular happiness she was in the possession of required, for the moment, not description but simply occupation — the quiet, accumulated, layered thing that she had described to Mr. Ashby on the common in the autumn, and which she now understood, rather better than she had then, as something that could be made as well as found.

"Check," said her father, moving his bishop.

"You always do that when I'm not attending," said Eleanor.

"You're attending," said Mr. Vane. "You are simply attending to two things at once, which has always been well within your capabilities." He looked at her over the board with the small, warm irony that was his particular idiom of love. "Are you happy, my dear?"

"I believe I am," said Eleanor. "Very nearly entirely."

"That is the best kind," said Mr. Vane. "The entirely happy have nothing to look forward to."

Eleanor moved her queen, and her father frowned, and the rain came down outside the library windows, and the fire burned steadily in the grate, and the books accumulated their long silence around them, and it was, in its modest and imperfect and entirely sufficient way, a very good afternoon.

CHAPTER XII

The weddings — for there were to be two, in the spring, which the neighbourhood considered a most efficient and satisfactory arrangement — were planned with the careful attention to detail that occasions of permanent consequence deserve. Margaret's wedding was to precede Eleanor's by a fortnight, and it was generally understood that the second ceremony would be the quieter of the two, since Eleanor had expressed a preference for simplicity and Mr. Ashby had expressed complete agreement, which Cecily said was the surest sign of a good marriage she had ever observed.

The months between the announcement and the spring brought their own small pleasures. Eleanor came to know Hatherswick Park as something other than a feature of the landscape — she walked its rooms with Mr. Ashby and they talked about how each might be used, a conversation that was practical on the surface and deeply interesting beneath it, since the choices people make about their domestic arrangements reveal more about their characters than almost any other kind of evidence. She found that his tastes and hers were not identical — he preferred larger rooms; she preferred more books — but that these differences were the generative kind, the kind that produce more interesting outcomes than either person would have achieved alone.

She came to know his seriousness more fully — the range of it, the things he cared about deeply and quietly, the commitments he had made to the tenants of the estate that reflected a genuine understanding of what the possession of land and income actually entailed. She came to know his humour, too, which ran parallel to hers in many places: dry, observant, not unkind, given to the particular pleasure of saying true things in surprising ways.

And she came to know, in the gradual and irreversible way of genuine intimacy, that she had been right. Not about everything — she had been wrong about several things, and expected to be wrong about many more, and was sufficiently self-aware to consider this a reasonable basis for a life shared with another reasonable person. But she had been right about the important thing: that certainty arrived not from the absence of doubt but from the presence of understanding, and that understanding — of another person, of oneself — was the work of a lifetime, which was exactly the right duration for it.

On the morning of her wedding, Eleanor stood at the window of her room at Whitmore Cottage and looked out at the valley, greening now in the early May light, the hedgerows coming into leaf, the hills soft and layered in the morning air. She thought of the many mornings she had looked at this same view, and of how the view had not changed and she had changed enormously, and of how strange and good it was that the world could remain so steady while the people in it were transformed.

"Eleanor," called Cecily from the corridor, "if you do not come down, Mama will have a full medical crisis."

"I am coming," said Eleanor, and turned from the window, and went downstairs to meet what came next.

A NOTE ON THE TEXT

This story has been composed in the manner, if not quite to the standard, of the great novelist whose spirit has been invoked throughout these pages. It is offered not as an imitation but as an homage — a small attempt to think about what she thought about: the careful negotiations between intelligence and feeling, the ways in which we deceive ourselves and are corrected by circumstance, and the quiet heroism of choosing, with full knowledge of one's own fallibility, to love something anyway.

The characters are invented. The observations are borrowed from everywhere.
